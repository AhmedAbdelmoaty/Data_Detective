––––––––––––––––––
CONTEXT
––––––––––––––––––

We are a professional training company building a serious game that teaches data analysis and analytical thinking through gameplay.

This is NOT a course.
This is NOT a quiz.
This is NOT an exam.
This is a GAME first.

Learning must happen implicitly, through player decisions, reasoning, exploration, and failure.

The player is a DATA ANALYST working inside a company, dealing with a real business problem (e.g., declining performance).
This is NOT a crime.
This is NOT an investigation.
All language, tone, and mechanics must reflect business problem-solving, not detective work or education.

––––––––––––––––––
CORE DESIGN PHILOSOPHY (CRITICAL)
––––––––––––––––––

The entire game must be built around HYPOTHESIS-DRIVEN THINKING.

This is the core of the experience:

• The player starts with multiple plausible hypotheses about the problem.
• Hypotheses appear early in the experience in some form (how they appear is your design choice).
• The player treats each hypothesis as a possible explanation — it may be correct or incorrect.
• The player explores the company, data, and people while mentally testing these hypotheses.
• Progression happens through ELIMINATION, not confirmation.

The player does NOT “find the right answer”.
The player gradually RULES OUT explanations by connecting evidence, analysis, and conversations.

The correct hypothesis is the one that remains after others have been reasonably eliminated — even if the player’s reasoning is imperfect.
Correctness is revealed ONLY after the final report.

––––––––––––––––––
ELIMINATION AS GAMEPLAY
––––––––––––––––––

Elimination is NOT a mechanical action.
Elimination is a RESULT of reasoning.

The player must feel that:
• “I tested this hypothesis”
• “I gathered information”
• “This explanation no longer fits the evidence I’ve seen”

How elimination is represented (state change, flow change, interaction, decision point) is a design choice — but elimination must always be supported by evidence from the player’s perspective.

There must be NO:
• automatic eliminations
• obvious labels (e.g., “this disproves X”)
• direct hints
• forced conclusions

The player should always understand WHAT they can do,
but never be told WHAT to conclude.

––––––––––––––––––
ROLE OF DATA, EVIDENCE, AND CONVERSATIONS
––––––––––––––––––

Data, evidence, and stakeholder conversations are NOT decorative content.

They are thinking tools.

• Evidence should be readable, business-oriented, and non-technical.
• Conversations should provide information, perspectives, or contradictions — never conclusions.
• Analysis reveals patterns, inconsistencies, or absences that matter.
• The player must connect these signals mentally.

Avoid technical overload.
Avoid explicit explanations.
Avoid “teaching moments”.

––––––––––––––––––
DESIGN CONSTRAINTS (MINIMAL BUT STRICT)
––––––––––––––––––

• This must feel like a real game.
• No quizzes, no exams, no multiple-choice tests.
• No explicit hints toward the solution.
• No detective or crime framing.
• Flow must be clear and intuitive — the player should never feel lost.
• Difficulty comes from reasoning, not navigation.
